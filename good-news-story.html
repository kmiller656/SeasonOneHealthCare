A hotline that never sleeps

A Season One story · Sample story — Recovery & crisis support

It's 3:12 a.m. when the line lights up. On the other end is a college student who hasn't slept in two days, describing thoughts she's never said out loud before. On this end is Marcus, five hours into an overnight shift, doing the thing he's done for six years: staying on the phone, staying calm, and staying present until she's safe.

"Most people think about crisis lines as this abstract, faceless service," Marcus says. "But it's just people. It's me, it's my coworkers, it's someone's neighbor, showing up at 2 a.m. because somebody needs to."

The shift nobody sees

The overnight crisis team works while most of the world sleeps — which is exactly when a lot of people need them most. Calls spike between midnight and 4 a.m., often from people who've spent the day holding it together and run out of daylight to do it in.

The team is small: four counselors on a given night, covering a call volume that would surprise most people. They rotate specialties — some are trained specifically in suicide risk assessment, others in substance use crises, others simply in the art of staying on the line with someone until the worst of it passes.

Why it works

What makes this particular team different isn't technology or funding — it's continuity. Most callers who phone back get connected with someone who remembers their situation. It's a small thing that changes the entire tone of a crisis call, from "explaining yourself to a stranger" to "picking back up with someone who already knows."

"You'd be amazed what it does for someone to hear, 'I remember you called last month, how did that job interview go,'" Marcus says. "It tells them they're not just a call. They're a person."

What they want people to know

  • You don't need to be in immediate danger to call — a bad night is reason enough.
  • Calling doesn't automatically trigger emergency services; the team's first job is to listen.
  • The overnight team takes care of each other too, with built-in debriefs after hard calls.

If you or someone you know is in crisis, you can call or text 988 any hour of the day — overnight included.
